## Abstract

MindSight is an AI pipeline that applies classical and modern artificial intelligence techniques to the Open Source Mental Illness (OSMI) Mental Health in Tech Survey dataset, covering seven years of responses from 2016 through 2022. The merged dataset comprises 3,433 survey rows and 193 columns, with the binary target variable `treatment` indicating whether a respondent sought professional mental health treatment. Across five sequential phases, the project deployed search algorithms, constraint satisfaction, propositional logic, and machine learning models to extract meaningful patterns from this complex, real-world dataset. The central finding of the machine learning phase is that the Delta Rule linear classifier achieved the highest test accuracy at 84.13%, outperforming a multilayer perceptron that reached only 71.32% despite the use of He initialization to counteract dead ReLU units. This result reflects the linear separability present in the preprocessed 18-feature space and challenges common assumptions that deeper models universally outperform simpler ones on small, tabular datasets. The broader implication is significant: AI techniques are not merely academic exercises but can provide actionable insight into the structural and organizational factors that drive mental health treatment behavior in technology workplaces, ultimately informing employer policy and workplace support design.

## 1. Introduction

Mental health in technology workplaces has become an increasingly urgent public health concern over the past decade. The software engineering profession is characterized by high cognitive load, extended work hours, demanding deadlines, and a cultural tendency toward stoicism and self-sufficiency that can discourage employees from acknowledging psychological distress or seeking professional support. The Open Source Mental Illness organization, known as OSMI, recognized this gap and began administering its annual Mental Health in Tech Survey in 2014. By gathering responses on topics ranging from employer awareness and stigma to personal treatment history and workplace policies, OSMI created one of the few longitudinal, domain-specific datasets on this subject available to researchers and practitioners alike.

Analyzing such a dataset manually is impractical. Survey responses are heterogeneous, spanning free-text fields, ordinal scales, and categorical indicators. Variables are missing at inconsistent rates across years. Column naming conventions shifted from year to year, reflecting questionnaire revisions. These characteristics make the dataset a compelling but challenging subject for automated analysis, and they justify the application of artificial intelligence techniques that can detect patterns at a scale and consistency that human analysts cannot match.

Artificial intelligence offers several distinct advantages in this context. Search algorithms can systematically explore the decision space defined by survey attributes, mapping paths from observed states to the target outcome of treatment-seeking behavior. Constraint satisfaction problem formulations can encode domain knowledge — such as plausible combinations of employer support and employee behavior — and use inference to reduce the space of valid solutions before any search begins. Propositional logic rules can express expert intuitions about the data in a transparent, verifiable form that allows practitioners to interrogate the model's reasoning process. Machine learning models, finally, can learn complex relationships from labeled examples and generalize to unseen respondents with measurable accuracy.

The central research question driving MindSight is whether these AI techniques, applied in a principled and sequential pipeline, can identify meaningful and statistically supported patterns in mental health treatment-seeking behavior among technology workers. Specifically, the project investigates which individual, organizational, and environmental variables serve as the strongest predictors of a respondent reporting that they sought professional treatment, and whether different reasoning paradigms — search, constraint, logic, and learning — converge on consistent findings about the domain. The answer to this question has practical value for employers, human resources professionals, and mental health advocates who wish to understand not just how common mental illness is in technology workplaces, but what conditions make it more or less likely that affected employees will pursue care.

## 2. Dataset Description

The MindSight dataset originates from the OSMI Mental Health in Tech Survey, which has been administered annually and made publicly available through Kaggle. Seven survey files were used, covering the years 2016 through 2022. Each file represents a unique cross-sectional snapshot of the technology workforce, with respondents recruited through social media, developer communities, and professional networks. The survey is self-administered and voluntary, meaning responses reflect self-selection bias to some degree, though OSMI's broad outreach strategy mitigates the most severe effects of this limitation.

Preprocessing began by loading all seven CSV files into individual pandas DataFrames and adding a `year` column to each before concatenating them into a single master DataFrame using `pd.concat`. This operation yielded a combined dataset of 3,433 rows and 193 columns. The high column count reflects the fact that column names were not perfectly consistent across survey years — questions were rephrased, reordered, or added, resulting in duplicate semantic concepts under different syntactic labels. A systematic renaming pass was performed to harmonize column names, mapping year-specific variants to canonical names shared across the pipeline.

Following renaming, columns with more than 50% missing values were dropped, as imputation would introduce more noise than signal for such sparsely populated fields. High-cardinality free-text columns, such as those capturing open-ended comments, were similarly removed because their content resists reliable encoding without natural language processing infrastructure beyond the scope of this project. The remaining columns were label-encoded using integer mappings for all categorical variables, ensuring compatibility with the numerical operations required by the machine learning phase. Feature values were then normalized to the zero-to-one range using min-max scaling, and the dataset was partitioned into an 80/20 train-test split. The final feature set comprised 18 columns, with training data shaped as (2,746, 18) and test data shaped as (687, 18). The binary target variable `treatment` has a positive class frequency of approximately 58%, representing a moderately imbalanced but workable distribution that did not require synthetic oversampling.

## 3. Search Algorithms

The search phase of MindSight constructed a graph from the survey data by treating unique values of key variables — primarily `work_interfere` (how much work is interfered with by mental health conditions) and `treatment` (whether the respondent sought professional care) — as nodes, and encoding co-occurrence patterns observed in the dataset as directed edges with associated path costs. The initial state for all search algorithms was the most frequently occurring value of `work_interfere`, which represents the modal self-reported level of work interference in the dataset. The goal state was defined as `treatment=1`, representing a respondent who sought professional help. By framing the problem this way, the search phase simulates the process of reasoning from an observed symptom profile toward a treatment outcome.

Five uninformed search strategies were implemented and evaluated. Breadth-first search (BFS) explores nodes level by level, guaranteeing that the shallowest goal state is found first. In the MindSight graph, BFS identified a path to the goal within a small number of levels, consistent with the graph's limited depth. Depth-first search (DFS) traverses as far as possible along each branch before backtracking, and on this small graph it also reached the goal quickly, though without the optimality guarantees of BFS. Uniform-cost search (UCS) extends BFS by ordering node expansion according to cumulative path cost, which makes it optimal when edge weights vary. Iterative deepening depth-first search (IDDFS) combines the memory efficiency of DFS with the completeness of BFS by repeatedly running DFS with progressively increasing depth limits, and it matched BFS in solution quality on this graph. Bidirectional search was also implemented, exploring simultaneously from both the initial state and the goal state and terminating when the two frontiers meet; this approach halved the effective search depth on the small graph.

Two informed search strategies were implemented using heuristic functions derived from domain knowledge. Greedy best-first search ranks nodes by estimated distance to the goal, providing fast but non-optimal solutions. A* search combines path cost with heuristic estimate, providing both completeness and optimality when an admissible heuristic is used. The heuristic used in MindSight was designed from the relationship between `work_interfere` severity and observed treatment rates in the dataset, assigning lower estimated costs to states associated with higher empirical treatment rates. A* outperformed greedy search in solution quality and remained efficient given the small graph size.

Three beyond-classical search techniques were also explored: hill climbing, simulated annealing, and beam search. Hill climbing is a local search method that greedily moves toward states with better heuristic values, making it fast but susceptible to local optima. Simulated annealing introduces probabilistic acceptance of worse states early in the search process to escape local optima, with the acceptance probability decreasing as the algorithm cools. Beam search retains only the top-k nodes at each level, trading completeness for memory efficiency. On the small MindSight graph, the distinction between these methods was less pronounced than it would be on larger problem spaces, but simulated annealing demonstrated the benefit of occasional uphill moves when the search landscape contained plateaus.

Two adversarial search strategies were implemented in a game-theoretic framing where one agent seeks to reach a treatment state and an opposing agent attempts to steer toward no-treatment states. Minimax search with full tree evaluation and alpha-beta pruning with branch elimination were both applied. Alpha-beta pruning achieved the same result as minimax while eliminating a significant fraction of evaluated nodes, demonstrating the efficiency gains achievable through intelligent branch pruning even in the mental health domain graph. All search algorithm outputs are documented with exact node counts and path lengths in the Phase 2 notebook; see `phase2/phase2_search.ipynb` output for the full algorithm comparison table.

The small size of the graph — arising from the limited number of unique `work_interfere` values and their treatment co-occurrence combinations — meant that all algorithms converged quickly and that differences in time complexity were not practically significant at this scale. The more important insight from the search phase is the structure of paths through the graph: the most direct routes to the treatment=1 goal consistently passed through states representing high work interference, suggesting that severity of work impact is a key discriminating feature, a finding that the logic and machine learning phases subsequently confirmed.

## 4. Constraint Satisfaction

The constraint satisfaction problem (CSP) formulation in Phase 3 modeled the decision-making context of a technology worker who may or may not seek mental health treatment. Five variables were defined to represent the key dimensions of this context. The variable `workplace_support` captures whether the respondent's employer provides mental health resources or support structures, with domain values reflecting the survey's available response options. The variable `treatment_sought` is a binary indicator of whether the respondent ultimately pursued professional care, serving as the primary outcome variable in the CSP. The variable `work_interfere` encodes the self-reported degree to which mental health conditions affect workplace productivity, with values ranging from Never through Rarely, Sometimes, and Often. The variable `remote_work` indicates whether the respondent works remotely, with domain values of Yes and No. Finally, `company_size` captures the size of the respondent's employer organization, bucketed into standard ranges drawn from the survey instrument.

Five constraints were defined to encode plausible and empirically grounded relationships among these variables. The first constraint specifies that when `work_interfere` takes the value Often, the probability of `treatment_sought` being Yes should be elevated, reflecting the empirical finding that severe work interference strongly correlates with treatment seeking. The second constraint encodes the opposite condition: when `work_interfere` is Never, the assignment of `treatment_sought=No` is strongly preferred. The third constraint links `workplace_support` and `treatment_sought`, requiring that if employer support is absent, the likelihood of treatment seeking decreases. The fourth constraint relates `company_size` and the availability of formal mental health resources, noting that smaller companies (1 to 5 employees) are less likely to provide structured support. The fifth constraint connects `remote_work` to the treatment outcome, reflecting survey findings that remote workers may have different access patterns to mental health resources.

Arc consistency-3 (AC-3) was applied prior to search to reduce variable domains by eliminating values inconsistent with the defined constraints. Under the condition that `treatment_sought` was pinned to zero — simulating the reasoning process for a no-treatment scenario — AC-3 removed two values from the domain of `work_interfere`: the values Often and Never were eliminated because they represented contradictory boundary conditions under the constrained treatment outcome. Often implies strong work interference that typically co-occurs with treatment seeking, making it arc-inconsistent with a no-treatment pin; Never implies negligible interference, which when combined with the absence of workplace support constraints, also failed the arc consistency check under specific constraint configurations. This domain reduction was a meaningful preprocessing step that simplified subsequent backtracking search.

Three backtracking search variants were evaluated on the CSP: standard backtracking without heuristics, backtracking with minimum remaining values (MRV) ordering, and backtracking with forward checking. Notably, all three variants required exactly two backtracks before reaching a consistent full assignment. The uniform backtrack count across all three variants suggests that the CSP is relatively well-structured and not highly sensitive to variable ordering heuristics at this scale. The problem has sufficient flexibility that the solver finds consistent assignments quickly regardless of which variable is assigned first, and the two backtracks encountered represent unavoidable constraint conflicts built into the domain structure rather than artifacts of poor search ordering.

The min-conflicts local search algorithm was also applied, beginning from a random initial assignment and iteratively selecting the variable involved in the most constraint violations and reassigning it to the value that minimizes remaining conflicts. Min-conflicts reached zero violations in just three iterations, a result that indicates the CSP is significantly under-constrained. An under-constrained problem is one where the constraints are collectively permissive enough that many valid assignments exist, making it easy for a local search method to find a solution without deep exploration. In the mental health domain context, this means that many combinations of the five variables are mutually consistent with the defined constraints, reflecting the genuine diversity of experiences and outcomes observed in the survey data.

## 5. Logic and Reasoning

The logic phase of MindSight translated domain insights from the dataset into a set of five propositional rules that express interpretable relationships between survey attributes and treatment-seeking behavior. Unlike the machine learning models, which learn implicit numerical representations, these rules operate as transparent, auditable if-then statements that can be directly read and evaluated by domain experts. Each rule was measured on two dimensions: support, which is the number of dataset rows matching the rule's antecedent conditions, and accuracy, which is the fraction of those rows where the rule's consequent matches the actual target label.

Rule R1 encodes the intuition that high work interference implies treatment seeking: if `work_interfere` is Often, then `treatment=1`. This rule fired on 65 instances and achieved an accuracy of 84.6%, confirming that severe work interference is a strong but imperfect predictor of professional help-seeking. Rule R2 encodes the opposite logic: if `work_interfere` is Never, then `treatment=0`. Despite firing on 120 instances, R2 achieved only 20.8% accuracy — the lowest of all five rules — revealing that the absence of reported work interference does not reliably predict that a respondent avoided treatment. This counterintuitive result likely reflects respondents who sought preventative or ongoing care independent of acute work interference, a common pattern in mental health treatment that this simple rule fails to capture.

Rule R3 combines the absence of employer support with lack of remote work to predict no treatment: if `seek_help=No` and `remote_work=No`, then `treatment=0`. This rule had the largest support at 172 instances but achieved only 45.9% accuracy, slightly below random chance, suggesting that this combination of factors alone is insufficient to predict treatment avoidance and that additional contextual variables would be needed to sharpen the prediction. Rule R4 targets small organizations: if `company_size` is 1 to 5 employees and `seek_help=No`, then `treatment=0`. With a support of 71 and an accuracy of 54.9%, R4 performed marginally better than chance, consistent with the hypothesis that small employers with no formal mental health resources slightly reduce treatment-seeking likelihood.

Rule R5 achieved the highest accuracy of all five rules at 88.7%, with a support of 71 instances. R5 states that if `work_interfere` is Sometimes and `seek_help=Yes`, then `treatment=1`. The superiority of R5 over R1 — despite R1 using a more severe interference level — demonstrates that combining moderate work interference with active employer support creates a stronger predictive signal than severity alone. This finding is substantively important: it suggests that employer-provided resources play a meaningful facilitating role in converting awareness of work impact into actual help-seeking behavior, even at moderate interference levels. The combination of environmental support and personal recognition of work impact appears to be the most reliable discriminator in the dataset.

Forward chaining was applied to the rule set in two scenarios. In the first run, the known facts included `work_interfere=Often`, which triggered rule R1 and derived the conclusion `treatment=1`. In the second run, the known facts included `work_interfere=Never` and `company_size=1-5` with `seek_help=No`, which triggered both R2 and R4 and derived `treatment=0`. These chaining runs demonstrate the rule system's ability to simulate a reasoning process analogous to how a clinician or analyst might walk through known facts and arrive at a conclusion. The system was also formulated in conjunctive normal form (CNF) to enable resolution-based theorem proving. CNF representation requires that all logical sentences be expressed as disjunctions of literals organized into clauses. In the mental health context, this allowed the rule set to be submitted to a resolution prover, which could confirm entailments — for example, verifying that given the known fact set of the first forward chaining run, the conclusion `treatment=1` is logically derivable from the axiom base, providing a sound and complete proof in addition to the heuristic forward chaining output.

## 6. Machine Learning

The machine learning phase of MindSight implemented five models entirely from scratch using NumPy, without relying on scikit-learn or any other machine learning library for the core learning algorithms. The 18-feature preprocessed dataset was split into training data of shape (2,746, 18) and test data of shape (687, 18). All five models were evaluated on the held-out test set, and their results reveal a nuanced picture of how model complexity interacts with dataset size and structure.

The Perceptron, the simplest of the three supervised classifiers, achieved a test accuracy of 66.67%. The Perceptron uses a hard step activation function, updating weights only when a misclassification occurs and providing no gradient information for correctly classified examples. This means the Perceptron converges only if the data is linearly separable, and on a dataset as complex and noisy as the OSMI survey, the step function's inability to provide fine-grained gradient signals limits how well the model can fit the training distribution. The result of 66.67% represents a meaningful improvement over the 58% majority class baseline but falls well short of more sophisticated approaches, confirming the Perceptron's theoretical limitations in practice.

The Delta Rule classifier, which uses a sigmoid activation function and minimizes mean squared error through gradient descent — effectively a logistic regression trained with gradient descent — achieved the highest test accuracy of all five models at 84.13%. This result is striking given that the Delta Rule is a linear classifier, operating with a single layer of weights and no hidden representations. The explanation lies in the structure of the preprocessed feature space: after label encoding, normalization, and feature selection, the 18 remaining features appear to capture the key discriminating signal in a form that is approximately linearly separable. On a dataset of this size (under three thousand training examples), a linear model with proper gradient training can find a decision boundary that generalizes well to the test set without the overfitting risk associated with more complex architectures.

The Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), despite its greater expressive capacity, achieved a test accuracy of only 71.32%, more than twelve percentage points below the Delta Rule. The MLP was implemented with one hidden layer and ReLU activations, trained via backpropagation over 200 epochs. Dead ReLU units — neurons that output zero for all inputs and cease to receive gradient updates — were identified as a problem during initial training, and He initialization was applied to the weight matrices to mitigate this issue by scaling initial weights according to the number of input units. Even with this correction, the MLP underperformed. The loss curve over 200 epochs showed initial rapid descent followed by a long plateau, suggesting that the model reached a local minimum relatively early in training and failed to escape it. This behavior is consistent with underfitting on a small dataset: the MLP's additional parameters provide flexibility that the limited training data cannot adequately constrain, and the model's solution is less generalizable than the simpler Delta Rule's decision boundary. Increasing the training dataset size, adding dropout regularization, or employing early stopping would likely improve MLP performance in a future iteration.

The two clustering algorithms, K-Means and K-Medoid, were evaluated using cluster purity as the performance metric, since the clustering task is unsupervised and ground truth labels are used only for post-hoc evaluation. Both algorithms were initialized with k=2 clusters, corresponding to the binary nature of the treatment target. Both K-Means and K-Medoid achieved identical purity scores of 58.05%. This figure is notable because it exactly matches the positive class frequency in the dataset — a result that indicates the clustering algorithms, at k=2, are essentially recovering the majority class cluster and failing to significantly discriminate beyond the base rate. K-Means and K-Medoid share this outcome because the clusters they discover in the 18-dimensional feature space are geometrically similar: the centroid-based and medoid-based representations of each cluster are close enough in this feature space that the cluster assignments diverge only marginally, if at all, across the two methods. The identical purity result is therefore not a coincidence but a reflection of the dataset's intrinsic cluster structure at this resolution — the natural groupings in the data do not align cleanly with the binary treatment label when all 18 features are considered simultaneously. Dimensionality reduction or targeted feature selection prior to clustering would likely improve purity in future work.

The differences expected to emerge across tracks are instructive to consider theoretically. Datasets that describe continuous physical processes — such as sensor readings, medical imaging measurements, or financial time series — tend to exhibit smooth, high-dimensional manifolds where the expressive capacity of deep neural networks translates into meaningful performance gains. In such settings, an MLP with sufficient capacity and regularization should outperform a linear classifier. The MindSight result, where the opposite occurred, reflects the particular character of tabular survey data: the features are categorical or ordinal, the number of samples is limited, and the relevant signal is concentrated in a small number of key interactions rather than distributed across thousands of feature dimensions. This reasoning suggests that tracks operating on datasets with similar tabular, categorical structure should observe MLP underperformance or parity with simpler classifiers, while tracks using image or sensor data should see deeper models win.

The clustering results are also expected to vary meaningfully across tracks. A dataset with well-separated natural clusters — for instance, one describing distinct user behavior segments — would produce much higher purity than the 58.05% observed in MindSight, where the binary target variable does not align cleanly with the geometric clusters formed in feature space. Tracks with datasets where categorical outcomes correspond to more distinct behavioral signatures should achieve purity scores significantly above the majority class baseline, providing a useful benchmark for comparing the discriminative power of the feature representations used across tracks.

From a CSP and logic perspective, the interesting comparative question is whether the rule-based systems derived from different tracks converge on interpretable patterns that domain experts recognize as valid. The MindSight finding that combining moderate work interference with active employer support (Rule R5) is a stronger predictor than severe interference alone (Rule R1) is a substantively meaningful result that a mental health researcher would recognize as plausible and policy-relevant. Whether analogous interpretable interactions emerge from other tracks will determine whether the propositional logic approach generalizes as a domain insight extraction tool across diverse datasets.

## 8. Conclusion

MindSight successfully demonstrated that a multi-paradigm AI pipeline — incorporating search algorithms, constraint satisfaction, propositional logic, and machine learning — can extract meaningful and interpretable insights from a complex, longitudinal survey dataset. The project's five phases each contributed distinct perspectives on the same underlying problem, and their findings were broadly consistent: the severity of work interference, the availability of employer-provided mental health resources, and the interaction between these two factors emerge repeatedly as the strongest predictors of treatment-seeking behavior among technology workers.

The most consequential quantitative finding is that the Delta Rule linear classifier achieved 84.13% test accuracy, outperforming the MLP's 71.32% on the 18-feature preprocessed dataset. This result challenges the intuition that more complex models are inherently superior and underscores the importance of matching model complexity to dataset size and structure. The CSP and logic phases reinforced this finding from different angles: the under-constrained nature of the CSP (zero violations in three min-conflicts iterations) and the interpretable strength of Rule R5 both point to a data landscape where a small number of well-chosen variables carry most of the predictive signal.

Looking back on the project, several improvements would be pursued given additional time and resources. The dataset would benefit from a more sophisticated preprocessing pipeline, including proper ordinal encoding for Likert-scale variables rather than simple label encoding, which imposes arbitrary ordinal structure on nominally categorical responses. The MLP architecture would be tested with dropout regularization, additional hidden layers, and early stopping to mitigate the underfitting observed in the 200-epoch training run. A more complex CSP with additional variables and tighter constraints would produce a more challenging and revealing constraint satisfaction problem, and more nuanced rule mining approaches — such as association rule learning with confidence and lift thresholds — would complement the five hand-crafted rules of Phase 4. Future work on this dataset could also include natural language processing of the free-text survey fields that were dropped during preprocessing, which likely contain rich qualitative insight into the experiences of technology workers with mental health conditions.
